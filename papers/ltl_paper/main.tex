\miniheading{Abstract}
We propose an assertional reinterpretation of the Functionality Cotyledon in A.\ Wayne Wymore's Tricotyledon Theory of System Design (T3SD). In classical T3SD, functional intent is verified constructively via a reference system $Z_{\text{spec}}$ and homomorphism $h: Z_{\text{impl}} \rightarrow Z_{\text{spec}}$. We define $\mathcal{FC}_{\mathcal{L}}(\Phi) = \{ Z \mid Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi \}$, where $\Phi$ is a set of temporal logic properties over Wymore discrete-system trajectories and $\mathcal{L}$ is a chosen dialect. The contribution is not a new model-checking engine but a Wymore-grounded FC definition, trace semantics, and workflow comparison. The framework is logic-agnostic at the theoretical level; FO-LTL and MSO provide an expressivity ceiling while tooling may restrict $\mathcal{L}$. We illustrate the comparison on a decentralized UAV swarm with infinite product state and dynamic participation change (Return to Base).

\section{Introduction}

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) promises improved traceability and earlier verification, yet many requirements remain in natural language or diagrammatic forms without checkable temporal semantics \parencite{salado2019mbse}. Validation then depends on manual reconstruction of intent: engineers cannot mechanically determine whether an implementation satisfies ``eventually complete search'' or ``always maintain separation'' without informal argument. Encoding requirements as temporal properties over explicit system trajectories is one path toward model-based requirements that are executable rather than documentary \parencite{salado2019mbse}.

A.\ Wayne Wymore's Tricotyledon Theory of System Design (T3SD) offers a rigorous foundation: cotyledon-structured design spaces, trajectory theorems, and constructive verification via reference systems and homomorphisms \parencite{Wymore1993,salado2019wymore}. Generic finite-state model checking lacks this design-space interpretation; our work stays within Wymore discrete systems (DSYSTEMS) and reinterprets the Functionality cotyledon assertionaly.

In classical T3SD, functional requirements are formalized as a specification system $Z_{\text{spec}}$, and correctness of an implementation $Z_{\text{impl}}$ is established by finding a system homomorphism $h: Z_{\text{impl}} \rightarrow Z_{\text{spec}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:comparison}A). Constructing and maintaining such morphisms is manual and poorly matched to evolving implementations, especially when concurrent subsystems induce large proof obligations \parencite{Wymore1993}. Consider a decentralized swarm of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs): when one vehicle executes \textbf{Return to Base (RTB)} after a battery fault and leaves the cooperative mission, participation in the active set changes. Maintaining universal constructive proofs across such transitions can require substantial re-verification even though mission intent may still be satisfiable overall.

We propose an assertional alternative: encode functional intent as a property set $\Phi$ in a temporal logic dialect $\mathcal{L}$ and verify implementations by checking $Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ over Wymore execution traces (Figure~\ref{fig:comparison}B). Assertional FC does not require $Z_{\text{spec}}$; our case study uses both readings on the same mission to compare workflows (Figure~\ref{fig:swarm-verification}).

\miniheading{Contributions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A parameterized assertional definition of the Functionality Cotyledon, $\mathcal{FC}_{\mathcal{L}}(\Phi) = \{ Z \mid Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi \}$, reframing functional verification as temporal property checking over Wymore trajectories rather than as construction of $Z_{\text{spec}}$ and $h$.
    \item Trace-based verification semantics for state and output trajectories $(g,y)$, aligned with Wymore's trajectory theorems \parencite{Wymore1993}, supporting model-based requirements executable against open-system boundaries \parencite{salado2019mbse}.
    \item A workflow comparison of constructive versus assertional verification on a decentralized UAV swarm with discrete-time ticks, continuous-valued state components, FO-LTL properties, and dynamic participation change under RTB.
\end{itemize}

The article proceeds as follows: Background summarizes T3SD, notation, and temporal logics; Methodology defines trace semantics, expressivity, verification workflows, and case study design; Results instantiates the swarm artifacts and reports verification outcomes; Discussion relates assertional FC to classical T3SD.

\section{Background}

\subsection{T3SD}
Wymore's T3SD organizes system design into three cotyledons (design spaces): Functionality, Buildability, and Implementability \parencite{Wymore1993}. The Functionality cotyledon contains system designs that satisfy required input/output behavior and preferred performance ordering. Buildability captures technology constraints; Implementability is the space of designs that homomorphically map buildable artifacts to functional ones. In classical verification, functional intent is realized constructively: system requirements are formalized into a reference system $Z_{\text{spec}}$, and implementations are checked via morphisms (Figure~\ref{fig:comparison}A). \textbf{This paper focuses on the Functionality cotyledon;} Buildability and Implementability are out of scope.

\subsection{Wymore Discrete Systems}
We restrict attention to Wymore's discrete systems (DSYSTEMS): a deterministic 5-tuple $Z = (S, I, O, \delta, \rho)$ with state set $S$, input set $I$, output set $O$, transition function $\delta$, and readout $\rho$ \parencite{Wymore1993}. Time index $t \in T$ advances in discrete, atomic ticks; state components may be continuous-valued at each tick (e.g., $x,y \in \mathbb{R}$), so ``discrete system'' refers to the clock, not to finite or integer-valued state. Verification in this paper evaluates properties at tick boundaries. Wymore explicitly allows $S$, $I$, and $O$ to be \emph{not-necessarily-finite}. Given an input trajectory $f: T \to I$ and initial state $s_0 \in S$, Wymore's trajectory theorems yield unique state and output trajectories $g: T \to S$ and $y: T \to O$. Engineered systems of interest here are open: verification is performed at the system boundary through observable inputs and outputs, consistent with Systems Theory \parencite{salado2019mbse}.

\miniheading{Notation}
Wymore, a proponent of Unicode, used uppercase multi-letter variable names (e.g., ITZ, STZ, OTZ, TZ). For consistency with standard mathematical presentation, this paper uses function notation; Table~\ref{tab:notation} summarizes the correspondence.

\subsection{Temporal Logics}
Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) expresses requirements over infinite execution traces using operators such as $G$ (always), $F$ (eventually), $X$ (next), and $U$ (until) \parencite{Pnueli1977,BaierKatoen2008}. Safety forbids bad states (e.g., $G\,\neg\text{collision}$); liveness requires progress (e.g., $F\,\text{fullCoverage}$). \textbf{First-Order LTL (FO-LTL)} extends propositional LTL with quantifiers over elements of $S$, $I$, or $O$ at each tick---needed when requirements relate continuous variables or indexed agents (as in the swarm case study). Signal Temporal Logic (STL) and Metric Temporal Logic (MTL) add continuous-signal constraints for cyber-physical systems \parencite{MalerNickovic2004}; TLA+ treats refinement as logical implication between temporal formulas \parencite{Lamport2002}. Functional intent in T3SD is inherently trajectory-based \parencite{salado2019mbse}.

\subsection{Related Formalisms}
Generic temporal model checking on finite automata does not, by itself, supply Wymore trajectory semantics, cotyledon interpretation, or a constructive-versus-assertional account of T3SD verification. Continuous-time hybrid verification (e.g., differential dynamic logic \parencite{platzer2018logical}) addresses dynamics \emph{between} discrete ticks; our framework evaluates properties at atomic Wymore clock steps. DEVS provides an event-scheduled simulation formalism complementary to Wymore's uniform time scale \parencite{wach2021devs}.

Assertional FC matters to MBSE practitioners as it connects checkable temporal requirements to Wymore's foundations and to model-based requirements practice \parencite{salado2019mbse} without requiring engineers to maintain reference systems and homomorphisms as implementations evolve.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Verification Paradigm Comparison}
Figure~\ref{fig:comparison} contrasts Wymore's classical constructive verification with the proposed direct assertional verification. In both panels, system requirements are formalized into a checkable artifact ($Z_{\text{spec}}$ or $\Phi$); the implementation $Z_{\text{impl}}$ is then verified against that artifact (morphism $h$ or property checking). Under the assertional paradigm, $\Phi$ is encoded in dialect $\mathcal{L}$ and checked by evaluating whether execution traces of $Z_{\text{impl}}$ satisfy each property in $\Phi$ \parencite{BaierKatoen2008}.

Functional requirements in T3SD are naturally expressed as input/output behavior over time \parencite{Wymore1993,salado2019mbse}. When implementations change structure, maintaining universal constructive proofs over $Z_{\text{spec}}$ and $h$ can require substantial re-verification. Assertional checking instead evaluates whether the post-change system satisfies $\Phi$ on its trajectories, without necessitating the construction of a new reference system.

\subsection{Formal Trace Definitions and Observability}
\label{sec:trace-observability}
Given a Wymorean system $Z = (S, I, O, \delta, \rho)$, an initial state $s_0 \in S$, and a complete input trajectory $f: T \to I$, Wymore defines unique state and output trajectories $g: T \to S$ and $y: T \to O$ \parencite[Def.~2.23, Thm.~2.29]{Wymore1993}. We write $g(t)$ for the state and $y(t)$ for the output at discrete time $t \in T$. The recursive form below is Wymore's standard trajectory construction:
\begin{align}
    g(0) &= s_0 \\
    g(t+1) &= \delta(g(t), f(t)) \\
    y(t) &= \rho(g(t))
\end{align}
We define the complete execution trace as $\sigma = (g(t), y(t))_{t \in T}$. Temporal requirements may use mixed observability:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{External (black-box):} $\Phi_{\text{ext}}$ over outputs $y(t)$ and inputs $f(t)$ only \parencite{salado2019mbse}.
    \item \textbf{Internal (white-box):} properties over state components in $g(t)$ (e.g., inter-agent separation).
\end{itemize}
We say $Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ if, for all admissible input trajectories $f$, the resulting trace satisfies $\sigma \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ under dialect $\mathcal{L}$ \parencite{Pnueli1977,BaierKatoen2008}. When $\mathcal{L}$ is clear we write $Z \models \Phi$. \textbf{Autonomous ticks:} engineered subsystems may advance via internal $\delta$ when no external command arrives; we treat such steps as ticks with hold-last-input or environment-defined $f(t)$. A complete axiomatization of autonomous-step liveness is deferred (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). Assertional and classical FC need not coincide: a morphism $h$ guarantees structural simulation; temporal properties on traces may under-specify internal structure or over-specify behavior via liveness clauses.

\subsection{Theoretical Expressivity vs.\ Computational Tractability}
\label{sec:expressivity}

Classical T3SD verification asserts \textbf{language inclusion}: every input/output trajectory of $Z_{\text{impl}}$ must be a valid trajectory within the universe defined by $Z_{\text{spec}}$. With infinite $S$ and arbitrary $\delta$, $Z_{\text{spec}}$ can encode rich behaviors. The assertional framework is intended to be at least as expressive as classical T3SD at the theoretical level.

\textbf{Theoretical definition.} $\mathcal{FC}_{\mathcal{L}}(\Phi)$ \textbf{admits} FO-LTL and richer dialects over trajectory variables at ticks; MSO-extended FO-LTL provides a ceiling for structural claims including morphism existence \parencite{Thomas1990}. More expressive logics remain admissible in the definition.

\textbf{Pragmatic restriction.} Tooling may restrict $\mathcal{L}$ to propositional LTL, bounded FO-LTL, or STL fragments for decidable checking (finite-state model checking, tick-local SMT). Propositional LTL alone is a \emph{tooling subset}, not the theoretical FC definition; the swarm case study requires FO quantifiers over agents and continuous coordinates.

\subsection{Discrete Trajectories and Verification Bounds}
This paper scopes verification to Wymore DSYSTEMS: trajectories evolve at discrete time steps according to $\delta$ and $\rho$, and $S$, $I$, and $O$ may be infinite \parencite{Wymore1993}. The satisfaction relation $Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ is defined over these trajectories regardless of cardinality.

When $|S|$, $|I|$, and $|O|$ are finite, standard finite-state model checking applies \parencite{BaierKatoen2008}. When one or more sets is infinite, automated checking requires abstraction, invariant generation, bounded prefix analysis, or theorem proving and decidability depends on the property fragment and domain.

\subsection{Constructive Verification Criteria}
\label{sec:constructive-criteria}
Classical FC seeks a system homomorphism $h : Z_{\text{impl}} \rightarrow Z_{\text{spec}}$ (Wymore Def.~4.3): surjective maps on states, inputs, and outputs that preserve $\delta$ and $\rho$ \parencite{Wymore1993}. Intuitively, every trajectory of $Z_{\text{impl}}$ is simulated by a corresponding trajectory in $Z_{\text{spec}}$. When $Z_{\text{impl}}$ is built from concurrent subsystems, Wymore's \textbf{conjunctive composition (CSY)} forms a product system with shared input ports and per-component readouts \parencite{Wymore1993}; the homomorphism obligation then relates the composed implementation to a (typically simpler) reference system.

\subsection{Case Study Design}
\label{sec:case-design}

We instantiate the methodology on one mission to compare constructive and assertional workflows under the same $Z_{\text{impl}}$, rather than to benchmark tool runtimes.

\miniheading{Mission and stressor}
A decentralized swarm of $N$ UAVs executes cooperative search over a bounded region $R \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ while maintaining minimum separation $d_{\text{safe}}$. \textbf{Return to Base (RTB)} removes a vehicle from the cooperative \emph{active mask} $M \subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$ when its battery falls below threshold. Participation change is the deliberate verification stressor: constructive proofs may require revisiting $h$, whereas assertional checking re-evaluates the same $\Phi$ on the suffix trace.

\miniheading{Artifacts and architecture}
Three artifacts share mission vocabulary at the boundary ports (Figure~\ref{fig:swarm-verification}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Assertional specification} $\Phi = \{\varphi_{\text{safe}}, \varphi_{\text{live}}\}$ in FO-LTL---the primary FC reading in this paper.
    \item \textbf{Implementation} $Z_{\text{impl}}$: a CSY composition of $N$ per-agent subsystems with continuous state at discrete flight-controller ticks.
    \item \textbf{Constructive reference} $Z_{\text{spec}}$ (optional): a behavioral aggregate over search progress, used to exhibit $h$ and compare verification \emph{effort}, not as a kinematic clone of each UAV.
\end{itemize}
Both FC readings apply to the same $Z_{\text{impl}}$: assertional checking of $\Phi$ (dashed path in Figure~\ref{fig:swarm-verification}) and constructive homomorphism to $Z_{\text{spec}}$ (double arrow).

\miniheading{Observability and properties}
Following Section~\ref{sec:trace-observability}, $\varphi_{\text{safe}}$ uses \textbf{white-box} state in $g$ (inter-agent separation over active pairs in $M$); $\varphi_{\text{live}}$ uses \textbf{black-box} mission outputs $y$ (\texttt{coveragePct} on \texttt{MissionOut}). Deadline semantics appear in $Z_{\text{spec}}$ readouts but are out of scope for $\Phi$ in this demonstration.

\miniheading{Rubric dimensions}
We assess whether the case study exercises: (1)~concurrent subsystems with infinite product state; (2)~continuous-valued state at discrete ticks; (3)~FO-LTL with mixed observability; and (4)~participation change under RTB as proof-maintenance for $h$, rather than as impossibility of classical T3SD.

\miniheading{Evaluation protocol}
Results proceed in five steps: (1)~define shared mission I/O and Wymore quintuples for $Z_{\text{spec}}$ and $Z_{\text{impl}}$; (2)~state $\Phi$ and the behavioral reference; (3)~give an illustrative mission I/O trace; (4)~compare constructive versus assertional handling of separation, liveness, and RTB (Tables~\ref{tab:comparison} and~\ref{tab:swarm-verification}); (5)~walk through an RTB vignette ($N=10 \rightarrow 9$) and map observations to the rubric dimensions above. This is a \textbf{conceptual workflow comparison}, not an empirical benchmark.

\miniheading{Mission Port Vocabulary}
Boundary types are shared by $Z_{\text{impl}}$, $Z_{\text{spec}}$, and black-box checking of $\Phi$ (Figure~\ref{fig:swarm-verification}).

\textbf{Input} \texttt{MissionCmd} (broadcast on one mission bus): \texttt{startSearch} sets phase $a \leftarrow \text{searching}$; \texttt{pause} holds the mission clock and sets $a \leftarrow \text{idle}$; \texttt{abort} terminates with $a \leftarrow \text{aborted}$; \texttt{tick} advances clock $t$ by one flight-controller cycle (default when no operator command is present).

\textbf{Aggregate output} \texttt{MissionOut} (swarm-level $\rho$ on both systems): \texttt{coveragePct} $\in \{0,\ldots,100\}$ is $100|K|/|\text{Cells}|$ capped at 100; \texttt{missionActive} is true iff $a = \text{searching}$; \texttt{deadlineMet} is true iff $t \leq T_{\max}$; \texttt{ack} is true iff $a \neq \text{aborted}$.

\textbf{Per-agent output} \texttt{AgentStatus} (one CSY status port per $Z_i$, implementation only): \texttt{active} is true iff agent $i \in M$; \texttt{rtbPending} is true iff $b_i$ is below the RTB threshold.

Mission phase $a \in \{\text{idle}, \text{searching}, \text{complete}, \text{aborted}\}$. In $\varphi_{\text{live}}$, $\text{Command}_{\text{search}}$ denotes issuance of \texttt{startSearch} on the mission bus.

\miniheading{Assertional Specification $\Phi$ (FO-LTL)}
We instantiate dialect $\mathcal{L}$ as FO-LTL. Functional intent is:
\begin{align*}
\varphi_{\text{safe}} &= G \bigl( \forall i,j \in M,\, i \neq j \rightarrow d_{ij} \geq d_{\text{safe}} \bigr) \\
\varphi_{\text{live}} &= G \bigl( \text{Command}_{\text{search}} \rightarrow F\, (\texttt{coveragePct} = 100) \bigr)
\end{align*}
where $d_{ij} = \sqrt{(x_i - x_j)^2 + (y_i - y_j)^2}$, $M$ is the active-agent mask, and $\varphi_{\text{live}}$ is evaluated on mission outputs $y$ (black-box), as specified in Section~\ref{sec:case-design}. Separation uses white-box state in $g$.

\miniheading{Requirement Encoding Example}
\begin{description}
    \item[Natural language:] Each active UAV shall maintain at least $d_{\text{safe}}$ from every other active UAV at all times.
    \item[FO-LTL:] $\varphi_{\text{safe}}$ above (quantifiers over active mask $M$).
\end{description}

\miniheading{Implementation $Z_{\text{impl}}$}
Each UAV is a Wymore subsystem $Z_i$; aggregate state is the product over $i \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$ sampled at discrete flight-controller ticks (e.g., 10~Hz):
\[
S_i = (x_i, y_i, \psi_i, v_i, b_i, \text{active}) \in \mathbb{R}^4 \times [0, 100] \times \{\top, \bot\}.
\]
Because coordinates are real-valued, $|S_i|$ and $|S_{\text{swarm}}|$ are infinite. The swarm is built by \textbf{conjunctive composition (CSY)}: broadcast \texttt{MissionCmd} on the mission bus, per-agent $\delta$ (kinematics and RTB clearing \texttt{active}), per-agent \texttt{AgentStatus} on status ports, and swarm-level $\rho$ aggregating \texttt{MissionOut} from projected cell coverage. RTB is encoded in $\delta$ when $b_i$ crosses threshold. Classical T3SD can also represent participation via mask $M$ in global state \parencite{Wymore1993}; a naive per-subset reference library scales as $2^N$, while a unified masked model avoids exponential system-definition count but leaves universal proofs non-trivial.

\miniheading{Constructive Reference $Z_{\text{spec}}$}
As previewed in Section~\ref{sec:case-design}, stakeholder intent is formalized as a \emph{behavioral} reference system---not a kinematic clone of each UAV (Table~\ref{tab:swarm-quintuples}). States record aggregate search progress $(K, t, a)$ where $K \subseteq \text{Cells}$ is the set of covered cell indices over $R$ (from active agents), $t$ is mission clock, and $a$ is phase. Shared mission ports are \texttt{MissionCmd} and \texttt{MissionOut}; $\rho$ maps $(K,t,a)$ to the four \texttt{MissionOut} fields defined above. Liveness in $Z_{\text{spec}}$ requires $K = \text{Cells}$ before $t > T_{\max}$ while $a = \text{searching}$. The same \texttt{coveragePct} field anchors $\varphi_{\text{live}}$ on the implementation side.

\miniheading{Two FC Readings on the Same Mission}
Figure~\ref{fig:comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:swarm-verification} realize the dual reading from Section~\ref{sec:case-design}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Classical FC (constructive):} $\exists h : Z_{\text{impl}} \rightarrow Z_{\text{spec}}$ (Section~\ref{sec:constructive-criteria}) with surjective state, input, and output maps preserving $\delta$ and $\rho$.
    \item \textbf{Assertional FC:} $Z_{\text{impl}} \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ on trajectories; $Z_{\text{spec}}$ is optional.
\end{itemize}

\miniheading{Illustrative Instance: RTB ($N=10 \rightarrow 9$)}
Following the evaluation protocol (Section~\ref{sec:case-design}), suppose UAV$_3$ enters RTB; agent~3 leaves $M$ while nine agents continue. \textbf{Constructive:} unless a universal mask invariant was proved upfront, RTB triggers a new proof obligation. \textbf{Assertional:} re-check $\Phi$ on the reduced trajectory; if $\varphi_{\text{safe}}$ and $\varphi_{\text{live}}$ hold over active agents and mission outputs, $Z_{\text{impl}} \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$.

\miniheading{Evaluation Rubric (Instantiated)}
The swarm confirms the rubric dimensions from Section~\ref{sec:case-design}: (1)~concurrent subsystems with infinite product state; (2)~continuous-valued state at discrete ticks; (3)~FO-LTL with mixed observability; and (4)~participation change under RTB as proof-maintenance for $h$, not impossibility of classical T3SD.

\section{Discussion}

Encoding functional requirements as temporal logic moves intent from natural-language statements into a checkable formal language \parencite{salado2019mbse,BaierKatoen2008}, addressing part of the gap toward \emph{true model-based requirements}---requirements whose semantics are explicit and executable against system trajectories rather than documentary. Assertional trace checking aligns with open-system validation themes in systems engineering practice \parencite{salado2019mbse}, though we do not claim normative alignment with any particular handbook process. When $\delta$ or $\rho$ change, engineers may re-check $Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ rather than rebuilding homomorphisms.

T3SD pedagogy traditionally introduces constructive FC first: students formalize $Z_{\text{spec}}$ and prove $h$ \parencite{Wymore1993}. Assertional FC is offered here as a \textbf{reinterpretation} of the same Functionality cotyledon for verification workflow---not a replacement of cotyledon structure or of classical T3SD when a reference system already exists. Wymore's allowance for not-necessarily-finite state and trajectory-based semantics supports FO-LTL at ticks as a dual reading of the same design space.

\subsection{Relationship to Classical Functionality Cotyledon}
\label{sec:classical-fc}

Classical T3SD defines functional designs constructively; assertional FC defines them by temporal properties in dialect $\mathcal{L}$. These are not equivalent in general (Section~\ref{sec:case-study}, Table~\ref{tab:swarm-verification}). A system homomorphism $h$ asserts language inclusion: trajectories of $Z_{\text{impl}}$ map into valid trajectories of $Z_{\text{spec}}$. Morphism existence implies trace inclusion under the homomorphic map---one direction of relationship. Conversely, $Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi$ does not imply existence of $h$ to a given $Z_{\text{spec}}$.

At the FO-LTL/MSO ceiling, $\mathcal{FC}_{\mathcal{L}}(\Phi)$ is \textbf{designed} not to shrink Wymore's classical specification universe; propositional LTL under-specifies relative to morphisms, while FO-LTL and MSO-extended FO-LTL aim for parity. Equivalence between assertional and classical solution spaces remains open. Constructive $Z_{\text{spec}}$ remains a valid reference for language inclusion when engineers prefer explicit behavioral models.

Constructive verification can still be preferable when $|S|$ is small, $Z_{\text{spec}}$ already exists, and changes are internal refinements with fixed observable behavior. In such cases, updating $h$ may be simpler than revising $\Phi$.

\subsection{Limitations and Open Questions}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Equivalence:} Assertional and classical FC are not proven equivalent; Tables~\ref{tab:comparison}--\ref{tab:swarm-verification} compare workflows, not logical strength.
    \item \textbf{Theory vs.\ tooling:} Theory admits FO-LTL/MSO; tooling may restrict $\mathcal{L}$; FO-LTL over reals is undecidable in general.
    \item \textbf{Infinite state:} The swarm may use tick-local SMT to check separation at sampled states---an under-approximation for inter-tick motion \parencite{BaierKatoen2008}; continuous-time between ticks is out of scope.
    \item \textbf{Constructive tractability:} Unified mask state avoids $2^N$ system definitions; universal proofs over all masks and real trajectories remain open.
    \item \textbf{Autonomous steps:} Complete liveness semantics for ticks without external input is deferred (Methodology).
    \item \textbf{Empirical / MBSE evidence:} No benchmark comparing morphism effort to property checking; no SysML requirements trace in this paper---integration with model-based requirements workflows is future work. The swarm case study is a conceptual demonstration.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions}

We reinterpret Wymore's Functionality Cotyledon assertionaly: $\mathcal{FC}_{\mathcal{L}}(\Phi) = \{ Z \mid Z \models_{\mathcal{L}} \Phi \}$, grounded in trajectories $(f,g,y)$ and compared against classical T3SD on a UAV swarm. FO-LTL and MSO remain admissible at the theoretical ceiling; practitioners may restrict $\mathcal{L}$ for tractable tooling. The swarm illustrates mixed observability, infinite product state, and verification across RTB participation change without rebuilding $Z_{\text{spec}}$ and $h$. Future work includes formal FC relationships, autonomous-step semantics, SysML trace integration, and tool-chain support. Formal mechanizations supporting this case study are available separately from the paper text.
